Reading Comprehension

The Amazon rainforest, often referred to as the "lungs of the Earth," plays a crucial role in regulating the global climate. It absorbs vast amounts of carbon dioxide and releases oxygen, making it essential for maintaining atmospheric balance.1 However, deforestation has been accelerating at an alarming rate, threatening to transform this vital ecosystem into a carbon source rather than a carbon sink.

Scientists have warned that the tipping point2 — the point beyond which the rainforest cannot recover — may be closer than previously thought. Recent studies indicate that approximately 17% of the Amazon has already been cleared for agriculture and cattle ranching3, with another 17% degraded by logging, fire, and drought.

The consequences extend far beyond South America. The Amazon's destruction would release billions of tons of stored carbon into the atmosphere, dramatically accelerating global warming4. Moreover, the loss of biodiversity would be catastrophic, as the rainforest is home to an estimated 10% of all species on Earth.

International cooperation is urgently needed to address this crisis. While Brazil has implemented programs to slow deforestation, the economic pressures facing local communities often undermine conservation efforts. Sustainable development strategies that balance environmental protection with economic needs remain the key to saving the Amazon.
